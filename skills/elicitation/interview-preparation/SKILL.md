# Interview Preparation

## Purpose

A well-prepared interview extracts more information in less time. This skill
guides you through research, agenda design, question crafting, and follow-up
planning so that every stakeholder conversation is productive.

## Procedure

### 1. Research the stakeholder

Before the interview:
- Review the stakeholder's role, expertise, and decision authority from the engagement model
- Check previous session transcripts for context already gathered
- Identify knowledge gaps this interview should fill
- Note any open questions assigned to this stakeholder

### 2. Design the agenda

Structure the session (typically 30-60 minutes):
1. **Opening (5 min)** — purpose, recording consent, rapport
2. **Context setting (5 min)** — recap what you already know, invite corrections
3. **Core elicitation (20-40 min)** — structured questions targeting gaps
4. **Validation (5 min)** — summarize back, check understanding
5. **Closing (5 min)** — next steps, follow-up items, thank

### 3. Craft questions

Use a mix of question types:
- **Open questions** — "Tell me about...", "Walk me through..."
- **Closed questions** — "Is this correct?", "Do you approve X?"
- **Probing questions** — "Why is that?", "What happens when...?"
- **Scenario questions** — "Imagine a user wants to..., what would they do?"

Order from general to specific (funnel technique). Save controversial or
sensitive questions for after rapport is established.

### 4. Prepare logistics

- Confirm time, duration, attendees, location/link
- Prepare recording setup (consent form if needed)
- Have the engagement glossary handy for shared vocabulary
- Prepare a parking lot for out-of-scope topics

### 5. Plan follow-up

After the interview:
- Send a summary within 24 hours
- Create work-items for any commitments made
- Update the engagement model with new information
- File follow-up questions as OpenQuestions

## Pitfalls

1. **Leading questions** ��� "Don't you think X is better?" biases responses. Use neutral framing.
2. **Scope creep** — Interviews can wander. Use the parking lot and gently redirect.
3. **Confirmation bias** — Don't only ask questions that confirm your hypothesis. Actively seek disconfirming evidence.
4. **Insufficient silence** — Allow pauses after questions. Stakeholders often share the most important information after initial silence.
5. **No follow-up** — An interview without a follow-up summary is wasted. Always close the loop.

## Verification

You prepared well if:
- [ ] You have 5-10 targeted questions (not generic)
- [ ] Questions map to specific knowledge gaps
- [ ] You allocated time for validation and parking lot
- [ ] You have a plan for capturing and distributing outcomes
- [ ] The stakeholder received the agenda in advance
